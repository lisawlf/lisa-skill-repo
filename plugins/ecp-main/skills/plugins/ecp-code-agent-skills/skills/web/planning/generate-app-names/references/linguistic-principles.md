<overview>
The psychology of naming draws from cognitive science, phonology, and neurolinguistics. Understanding how the brain processes and remembers names enables strategic sound and structure selection.
</overview>

<cognitive_fluency>

<principle>
The human brain naturally gravitates toward information requiring minimal cognitive effort. Names that are easy to process are more likely to be remembered, liked, and trusted.
</principle>

<optimal_length>
Research indicates names between **6-8 characters** perform best in memory tests:
- Short enough for immediate recall
- Long enough for distinctiveness
- Fits comfortable speech patterns
- Easy to type and search

**Examples at optimal length:**
- Google (6)
- Spotify (7)
- Netflix (7)
- Twitter (7)
- Amazon (6)
</optimal_length>

<fluency_factors>
- **Phonetic simplicity**: Common sound combinations that feel natural
- **Spelling transparency**: Name spelled as it sounds
- **Syllable structure**: 2-3 syllables ideal for English speakers
- **Familiar patterns**: Uses recognizable word structures
- **Pronounceability**: Can be said without hesitation
</fluency_factors>

</cognitive_fluency>

<sound_symbolism>

<bouba_kiki_effect>
Humans associate specific sounds with shapes and concepts:
- **Rounded sounds** (m, l, b, o, u) → soft, curved, gentle
- **Sharp sounds** (k, t, p, i) → angular, precise, energetic

This cross-modal association is universal across cultures and influences name perception before conscious processing.
</bouba_kiki_effect>

<phonetic_categories>

<category name="plosives">
**Sounds:** /k/, /t/, /p/, /b/, /g/, /d/

**Psychological associations:**
- Strength and power
- Precision and impact
- Energy and dynamism
- High recall and memorability

**Brand examples:**
- **K**oda**k** (double plosive)
- **G**oo**g**le (voiced plosives)
- Fe**d**E**x** (sharp ending)
- **K**it**K**at (alliterative plosives)
- **T**ar**g**e**t** (multiple plosives)

**Best for:** Tech products, performance brands, action-oriented services
</category>

<category name="fricatives_nasals">
**Sounds:** /m/, /n/, /l/, /s/, /f/, /v/, /z/

**Psychological associations:**
- Warmth and comfort
- Gentleness and smoothness
- Fluidity and elegance
- Nurturing and soft

**Brand examples:**
- **M**&**M**'s (nasal consonants)
- **N**est**l**é (soft consonants)
- **L**u**l**u**l**e**m**on (liquid consonants)
- **S**i**l**k (fricative + liquid)
- **V**e**l**vet (soft beginning)

**Best for:** Comfort products, wellness brands, luxury items, family-oriented services
</category>

<category name="long_vowels">
**Sounds:** /aɪ/ (as in "sky"), /eɪ/ (as in "day"), /oʊ/ (as in "go")

**Psychological associations:**
- Sophistication and elegance
- Luxury and premium quality
- Expansiveness and aspiration
- Openness and possibility

**Brand examples:**
- N**i**ke (/aɪ/)
- H**y**att (/aɪ/)
- **A**mazon (/eɪ/)
- Fac**e**book (/eɪ/)

**Best for:** Premium brands, aspirational products, luxury services
</category>

<category name="repetitive_vowels">
**Sounds:** /uː/ (as in "oo"), doubled vowels

**Psychological associations:**
- Roundness and completeness
- Playfulness and fun
- Approachability
- Digital/modern feel

**Brand examples:**
- G**oo**gle
- Yah**oo**
- H**u**l**u**
- Z**oo**m
- V**oo**d**oo**

**Best for:** Consumer apps, entertainment, social platforms, playful brands
</category>

</phonetic_categories>

<phonaesthemes>

<definition>
Phonaesthemes are sound clusters that carry inherent meaning due to patterns across many words in a language.
</definition>

<common_phonaesthemes>
| Cluster | Association | Words | Brand Applications |
|---------|-------------|-------|-------------------|
| gl- | Light, vision | glimmer, glow, gleam, glisten | Glamour, Gleam |
| sl- | Sliding, smooth | slide, slick, sleek, slim | Sleek, Slimfast |
| sn- | Nose, quick action | sniff, snap, snare, snatch | Snapchat, Snickers |
| fl- | Flying, flowing | fly, flow, flutter, flame | Flickr, Flow |
| sw- | Sweeping motion | sweep, swing, sway, swim | Swift, Swipe |
| cr- | Breaking, sharp | crack, crash, crush, crisp | Crunch, Crisp |
| sp- | Expulsion, spray | spit, spray, spark, spin | Spotify, Spark |
| tw- | Light motion | twist, twirl, twitter, twinkle | Twitter, Twitch |
| str- | Strength, stretch | strong, stretch, stride, strike | Stripe, Stream |
</common_phonaesthemes>

<strategic_application>
Use phonaesthemes to anchor invented names in familiar meaning patterns:
- A light/vision product → gl- prefix
- A fast/quick action tool → sn- or sp- prefix
- A flowing/smooth experience → fl- or sl- sounds
- A powerful/strong platform → str- or cr- sounds
</strategic_application>

</phonaesthemes>

<rhythmic_patterns>

<alliteration>
Repeating initial consonant sounds creates catchiness and memorability.

**Examples:**
- Coca-Cola
- PayPal
- Best Buy
- Dunkin' Donuts
- TikTok (also reduplication)

**Effect:** Playful, memorable, rhythmic, easy to say
</alliteration>

<assonance>
Repeating vowel sounds within words creates internal harmony.

**Examples:**
- YouTube (oo-oo)
- Shake Shack (a-a)

**Effect:** Smooth, flowing, pleasant sound
</assonance>

<reduplication>
Repeating syllables or sounds for emphasis and memorability.

**Examples:**
- TikTok
- Tic Tac
- Kit Kat
- Jamba Juice

**Effect:** Highly memorable, playful, rhythmic
</reduplication>

</rhythmic_patterns>

<emotional_triggers>

<naming_approaches>

<approach name="emotional">
Names that connect through feelings like pride, joy, nostalgia, or excitement.

**Examples:**
- Joy (dishwashing)
- Triumph (motorcycles)
- Bliss (spa services)
- Delight (software)

**Technique:** Use abstract emotion words or evoke emotional states indirectly.
</approach>

<approach name="experiential">
Names based on the experience of using the product, creating neuro-associations.

**Examples:**
- Safari (exploration experience)
- Kindle (igniting imagination)
- Steam (immersive gaming)
- Nest (home comfort)

**Technique:** Identify the core emotional experience and find words that evoke it.
</approach>

<approach name="associative">
Names that connect to a specific quality or feature consumers desire.

**Examples:**
- Zoom (speed, efficiency)
- Slack (reducing work friction)
- Buffer (smoothing social media)
- Notion (ideas and thinking)

**Technique:** Identify the key benefit and find related concepts.
</approach>

</naming_approaches>

</emotional_triggers>

<memory_optimization>

<factors_improving_recall>
1. **Distinctiveness**: Unique sounds stand out in memory
2. **Concreteness**: Concrete nouns (Apple) beat abstract concepts
3. **Emotional valence**: Names with positive associations remembered better
4. **Personal relevance**: Names connecting to user identity or goals
5. **Repetition exposure**: Consistent usage reinforces memory
</factors_improving_recall>

<structural_recommendations>
- Begin with a strong consonant (easier recall than vowel starts)
- End with a vowel for smooth pronunciation
- Use stressed syllables early (TRIumph, GOOgle)
- Avoid consonant clusters that trip pronunciation (strength, twelfths)
- Maintain phonetic consistency (don't mix incompatible sounds)
</structural_recommendations>

</memory_optimization>
