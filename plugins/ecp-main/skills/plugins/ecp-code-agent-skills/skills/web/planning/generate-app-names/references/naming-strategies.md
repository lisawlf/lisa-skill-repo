<overview>
All brand names fall into distinct taxonomic categories, each with strategic advantages and risks. Selecting the right approach depends on market positioning, competitive landscape, and long-term brand goals.
</overview>

<strategy_taxonomy>

<strategy name="descriptive">
<definition>
Names that explicitly state what the product or service does.
</definition>

<examples>
- General Motors
- International Business Machines (IBM)
- The Weather Channel
- PayPal
</examples>

<advantages>
- Immediate clarity and recognition
- SEO benefits for category terms
- Low marketing investment needed for comprehension
- Clear value proposition communication
</advantages>

<disadvantages>
- Difficult to trademark (generic terms)
- Limits company expansion into new categories
- Vulnerable to competition using similar descriptive terms
- Can become dated if product evolves
</disadvantages>

<ideal_for>
- B2B companies with specific functional offerings
- Niche products with clear singular purpose
- Utility services where clarity trumps memorability
- Products where search discoverability is critical
</ideal_for>
</strategy>

<strategy name="suggestive">
<definition>
Names that hint at benefits or qualities through metaphors, analogies, or evocative language without directly describing the product.
</definition>

<examples>
- Spotify (spot + identify music)
- FitBit (fitness + bit of technology)
- Pinterest (pin + interest)
- Slack (organizational slack time)
</examples>

<advantages>
- Sparks curiosity and engagement
- More memorable than descriptive names
- Generally more trademarkable
- Allows brand personality expression
- Provides flexibility for product evolution
</advantages>

<disadvantages>
- Risk of being too vague or confusing
- Requires some marketing to establish connection
- May not translate well across cultures
- Can be misinterpreted
</disadvantages>

<ideal_for>
- Technology startups
- Lifestyle and entertainment brands
- Consumer apps and services
- Products targeting younger demographics
</ideal_for>
</strategy>

<strategy name="evocative">
<definition>
Names using poetic or imaginative language to create a mood, feeling, or association without any direct product connection.
</definition>

<examples>
- Amazon (vastness, exploration, abundance)
- Apple (simplicity, freshness, knowledge)
- Virgin (purity, newness, unconventionality)
- Patagonia (adventure, wilderness, authenticity)
</examples>

<advantages>
- Strong emotional and aspirational connections
- Highly memorable and distinctive
- Excellent brand storytelling potential
- Maximum flexibility for business expansion
- High trademark defensibility
</advantages>

<disadvantages>
- Can initially confuse consumers about product category
- Requires significant marketing investment
- Success depends heavily on execution
- Risk of appearing pretentious if poorly executed
</disadvantages>

<ideal_for>
- Premium and luxury brands
- Aspirational lifestyle products
- Companies planning significant diversification
- Brands prioritizing emotional connection over clarity
</ideal_for>
</strategy>

<strategy name="abstract_invented">
<definition>
Completely made-up words with no pre-existing meaning, serving as blank slates for brand building.
</definition>

<examples>
- Kodak
- Exxon
- Xerox
- Häagen-Dazs
- Accenture
</examples>

<advantages>
- Total trademark control and protection
- Unique identity with no competitor similarity
- No negative pre-existing associations
- Excellent for global markets (no translation issues)
- Maximum creative freedom
</advantages>

<disadvantages>
- Requires heavy marketing investment to build meaning
- No inherent memorability hooks
- Risk of seeming cold or corporate
- Harder to spell and remember initially
</disadvantages>

<ideal_for>
- Global brands needing universal appeal
- Companies in crowded competitive spaces
- Innovative categories defining new markets
- Organizations with substantial marketing budgets
</ideal_for>
</strategy>

<strategy name="hybrid_compound">
<definition>
Names combining elements of different words (portmanteaus) or using two complete words together to create new identities.
</definition>

<examples>
**Portmanteaus:**
- Netflix (net + flicks)
- Instagram (instant + telegram)
- Microsoft (micro + software)
- Pinterest (pin + interest)

**Compound Words:**
- Facebook
- Snapchat
- YouTube
- WordPress
</examples>

<advantages>
- Creates unique, ownable identity
- Maintains some semantic meaning
- Generally trademarkable
- Can be highly memorable and clever
- Combines benefits of multiple concepts
</advantages>

<disadvantages>
- Risk of sounding gimmicky or dated
- May be confusing if blend is unclear
- Can be difficult to pronounce
- Some blends feel forced
</disadvantages>

<ideal_for>
- Consumer apps and digital platforms
- Social media and communication tools
- Tech startups wanting modern identity
- Products combining two distinct value propositions
</ideal_for>
</strategy>

<strategy name="founder_personal">
<definition>
Names based on the founder's name or a fictional character/persona.
</definition>

<examples>
- Ford (Henry Ford)
- Disney (Walt Disney)
- Chanel (Coco Chanel)
- Dell (Michael Dell)
- Betty Crocker (fictional persona)
</examples>

<advantages>
- Conveys authenticity and heritage
- Builds personal trust and connection
- Unique and inherently trademarkable
- Strong storytelling foundation
</advantages>

<disadvantages>
- Scalability challenges (personal reputation tied to brand)
- Succession and exit complications
- May limit perceived company scope
- Personal scandals affect brand
</disadvantages>

<ideal_for>
- Artisan and craft businesses
- Professional services (law, consulting)
- Family-owned enterprises
- Luxury goods with heritage emphasis
</ideal_for>
</strategy>

<strategy name="acronym_initialism">
<definition>
Names formed from initial letters or abbreviations.
</definition>

<examples>
- IBM (International Business Machines)
- BMW (Bayerische Motoren Werke)
- IKEA (Ingvar Kamprad Elmtaryd Agunnaryd)
- UPS (United Parcel Service)
</examples>

<advantages>
- Short and professional
- Versatile across contexts
- Can evolve beyond original meaning
- Works in formal business environments
</advantages>

<disadvantages>
- Lacks inherent meaning or emotion
- Harder to remember without context
- Difficult to differentiate (many similar acronyms)
- Pronunciation can vary globally
</disadvantages>

<ideal_for>
- Technical and B2B companies
- Government and institutional organizations
- Companies whose full name is too long
- Established brands simplifying identity
</ideal_for>
</strategy>

</strategy_taxonomy>

<strategy_selection_matrix>
| Factor | Best Strategy |
|--------|---------------|
| Need immediate clarity | Descriptive |
| Targeting creative/young audience | Suggestive or Hybrid |
| Premium positioning | Evocative |
| Global expansion planned | Abstract/Invented |
| Crowded competitive space | Abstract or Evocative |
| Limited marketing budget | Descriptive or Founder |
| Building personal brand | Founder/Personal |
| Tech/digital product | Suggestive or Hybrid |
| B2B enterprise | Descriptive or Acronym |
| Consumer lifestyle | Evocative or Suggestive |
</strategy_selection_matrix>

<strategic_recommendations>

<recommendation name="think_long_term">
Consider 3-5 years ahead. Will the name support future line extensions? Can the business pivot without the name becoming an obstacle? Avoid names that pigeonhole the business into a narrow category.
</recommendation>

<recommendation name="prioritize_differentiation">
In crowded markets, differentiation matters more than clarity. A unique name that requires some explanation is often more valuable than a descriptive name that blends with competitors.
</recommendation>

<recommendation name="test_multiple_approaches">
Generate candidates across 2-3 different strategy types. The best name may come from an unexpected category. Don't commit to one approach before exploring alternatives.
</recommendation>

</strategic_recommendations>
